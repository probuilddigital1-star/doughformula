Forget salt in a bread formula and the result is immediately obvious. The dough ferments faster than expected, the crust comes out pale rather than deep brown, the crumb is oddly open in places and weak overall, and the flavor is flat in a way that no amount of good fermentation can fix. Salt is doing several jobs at once, and you can feel the absence of each one.

## Salt Controls Fermentation

This is the function that surprises bakers most when they first learn it. Salt slows yeast activity by pulling water out of yeast cells through osmosis. With less free water available, the yeast metabolizes sugars more slowly and produces CO2 at a reduced rate.

That sounds like a drawback, but it is the opposite. Controlled, slower fermentation produces a more complex flavor profile. Yeast has more time to produce a wider range of aromatic compounds. Lactic acid bacteria in sourdough work at a pace that builds acidity in proportion to the overall fermentation, rather than racing ahead. The pacing that salt creates is part of why a properly salted, long-fermented bread tastes more complex than a fast, unsalted one.

The practical implication: if you accidentally omit salt, expect your dough to ferment significantly faster. A bulk fermentation that normally takes four hours might be ready in two and a half. This also explains why bakers who add salt at the very end of mixing (after autolyse) see a small window of faster fermentation before the salt is fully incorporated.

## Salt Strengthens Gluten

Salt ions interact with gluten proteins in a way that tightens the overall network. A salted dough is firmer, more elastic, and holds gas more effectively than the same dough without salt. You can feel this difference directly: mix flour and water into a shaggy mass, then fold in salt after five minutes. The dough firms up noticeably within the first few folds.

This is why the timing of salt addition can affect dough character. In autolyse, bakers intentionally leave out salt (and often yeast or starter) to let gluten develop in a more extensible, relaxed state before salt tightens things up. This gives the flour time to hydrate and form gluten bonds before the restrictive effect of salt kicks in.

For most home bakers, salt goes in early in the process and this is fine. The autolyse technique is a refinement rather than a requirement. The [autolyse guide](/techniques/autolyse-step-by-step/) covers when the timing actually matters.

## How Salt Affects the Crust

Salt is hygroscopic, meaning it attracts and holds moisture from the air. In a baked loaf, salt helps the crust retain a subtle chewiness after cooling, rather than going completely brittle. It also affects Maillard browning, the chemical reaction between amino acids and sugars that produces crust color. A salted loaf browns more readily and develops a darker, more appetizing crust than an unsalted one baked in the same conditions.

Bakers who operate at high altitude or in very low humidity sometimes note that their crusts are more brittle than expected. Salt content is one of the variables worth examining, alongside steam and baking temperature.

## Getting the Percentage Right

The standard range for salt in bread is 1.8% to 2.2% by baker's percentage (that is, 1.8 to 2.2 grams of salt per 100 grams of flour). Most commercial bakeries land around 2%. Home bakers often go slightly lower, at 1.8% to 1.9%, while some artisan loaves push to 2.2% for a more pronounced crust and firmer crumb.

Below 1.5%, the fermentation control and structural benefits start to diminish noticeably. The dough ferments faster than expected, the crumb can be erratic, and flavor lacks depth.

Above 2.5%, salt begins to inhibit fermentation significantly. Yeast activity can slow to the point where the dough rises very slowly or requires much more time than planned. Some flatbreads use high-salt formulas deliberately to slow fermentation for flavor development, but for standard yeasted loaves, staying at or below 2.2% keeps the balance functional.

## Salt Type and Measurement

This is where a common mistake lives. Table salt and fine sea salt are not interchangeable by volume, and even by weight they can differ because of grain size and additives.

Kosher salt is the clearest example. Diamond Crystal kosher salt is significantly lighter by volume than Morton kosher salt because of different crystal structures. One tablespoon of Diamond Crystal weighs roughly 5 grams, while one tablespoon of Morton weighs roughly 8 grams. If a recipe specifies "kosher salt" by volume without naming the brand, you could be off by more than 50%.

The solution is to measure salt by weight and ignore volume measurements. This is one of the strongest arguments for using a scale for all bread ingredients. At 2% salt, the difference between 12 grams and 18 grams of salt in a 1000-gram flour batch is the difference between a well-fermented loaf and a sluggish one.

Table salt often contains iodine and anti-caking agents. Iodine can inhibit yeast. At the concentrations found in commercially iodized salt, this effect is small but present. For most baking, the practical impact is minor, but if you are troubleshooting unexpectedly slow fermentation and are using heavily iodized table salt, it is worth trying non-iodized salt.

Fine sea salt by weight is equivalent to table salt for baking purposes, assuming no additives. Flaked sea salt like Maldon weighs less per tablespoon than either and should always be measured by weight.

## Common Mistakes

The most frequent mistake is omitting salt by accident when baking sourdough. Some bakers forget it during an autolyse and then skip adding it after because the dough feels "done." The result is a flat, pale, rapidly over-fermented loaf. If you realize partway through bulk that you forgot salt, you can still add it. Dissolve the salt in a small amount of the dough's water, then work it in through folds. It will incorporate, though not as evenly as if it had gone in at the start.

The second common mistake is swapping salt types by volume. If your formula says "10 grams of salt" and you weigh it, you are fine regardless of salt type. If it says "2 teaspoons" and you are using a different salt than the recipe author, you may be under or over by a significant margin.

A subtler mistake is not accounting for salt when designing a very fast fermentation schedule. Some quick bread recipes use high yeast amounts and expect the dough to double in forty-five minutes. Salt at 2% will slow that process. If you are working from a timed formula without understanding why the timing works, adding more salt to "improve flavor" can push the dough outside the window the recipe was built around.

Finally, some bakers keep their salt content very low to make the bread taste milder, not realizing that the structural and fermentation effects will also change. Lower salt means faster fermentation, weaker gluten, and lighter crust color. These are the right tradeoffs for specific bread styles. For most standard loaves, staying in the 1.8% to 2% range keeps all the systems working together.

## The Salt and Fermentation Connection

Salt, fermentation speed, and flavor are tightly linked. If you want to understand how all three interact, the [fermentation guide](/fundamentals/what-fermentation-actually-does/) covers what yeast and bacteria are doing at different temperatures and salt levels.

Once you have your salt percentage dialed in, the rest of the formula tends to come together more predictably. It is one of the few ingredients where a small change in percentage produces a visible, measurable change in the finished loaf.
